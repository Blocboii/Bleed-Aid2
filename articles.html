The uptake of ALTUVIIIO, a novel once-weekly Factor VIII replacement therapy, appears promising but should be considered alongside existing treatment options.

Unlike Emicizumab, which provides continuous coverage via a biweekly or once monthly subcutaneous injection, ALTUVIIIO offers higher-for-longer levels of Factor VIII, reaching near-normal to normal ranges for most of the week[2].

In a Phase 3 study, ALTUVIIIO reduced the annualized bleeding rate (ABR) by 77% compared to previous prophylaxis, while achieving a mean ABR of 0.70[1].

However, the convenience of subcutaneous administration with Emicizumab might still appeal to some patients despite the need for twice-monthly injections.

*Ultimately, the choice between ALTUVIIIO and Emicizumab depends on individual preferences regarding administration method, treatment goals, and potential risks such as the development of inhibitors[5]. *

Rebalancing Agent - [Concizumab]


Rebalancing agent. New kid on the block Concizumab

Concizumab is a novel investigational drug currently being developed for the treatment of hemophilia A and hemophilia B. It is a monoclonal antibody that targets and inhibits tissue factor pathway inhibitor (TFPI), a protein that regulates blood clotting.

Here are some key points to know about concizumab:

1. Mechanism of action: Concizumab works by blocking TFPI, which is a natural inhibitor of the blood coagulation process. By inhibiting TFPI, concizumab can potentially enhance blood clotting and reduce bleeding episodes in patients with hemophilia.

2. Hemophilia A and B: Concizumab has shown promise in both types of hemophilia.

3. Clinical trials: Concizumab has undergone several clinical trials to evaluate its safety and efficacy. [25/02, 10:46 am] null: Clinical trials: Concizumab has These trials have included both adults and children with hemophilia A or B. Results from early studies have shown a reduction in bleeding episodes and an improvement in clotting factors.

4. Potential benefits: If approved, concizumab could offer a new treatment option for patients with hemophilia A or B. It has the potential to reduce bleeding episodes, improve quality of life, and reduce the need for factor replacement therapy.

5. Safety considerations: As with any medication, concizumab has potential side effects. Common side effects observed in clinical trials include injection site reactions, headaches, and joint pain. Long-term safety data is still being evaluated.

It's important to note that concizumab is still under investigation and has not yet been approved by regulatory authorities. Patients who are interested in concizumab should consult with their healthcare provider for the latest information and updates on its development.
